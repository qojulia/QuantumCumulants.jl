# Closest existing work to CumulantHomotopy

This document collects the research directions that come closest to the CumulantHomotopy objective:

> Compute the physically selected stationary state of a truncated quantum cumulant hierarchy while keeping the number of actively tracked roots small, preserving as much information as possible from the original Liouvillian, and diagnosing convergence toward the unique stationary state.

No single existing method appears to solve the complete problem. The closest precedents divide naturally into five roles:

1. stationary moment collapse and uniqueness testing,
2. quantum moment positivity and certification,
3. repair of truncated hierarchies,
4. state-space truncations that preserve a unique linear stationary problem,
5. high-order candidate generation and continuation.

The most promising CumulantHomotopy architecture is a synthesis of these ideas.

---

## 1. Closest overall quantum method

### Robichon and Tilloy: stationary-state bootstrap for bosonic open systems

**Reference**

G. Robichon and A. Tilloy,  
“Bootstrapping the stationary state of bosonic open quantum systems” (2024).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.07384

### Main idea

The method combines exact stationary identities

\[
\langle \mathcal L^\dagger O\rangle = 0
\]

with positivity of moment matrices

\[
M_q(y)\succeq 0.
\]

At increasing operator order, this produces progressively tighter lower and upper bounds on stationary observables.

### Why it is close to CumulantHomotopy

It already works with:

- bosonic open quantum systems,
- stationary expectation values,
- the original Liouvillian,
- truncated moment data,
- quantum positivity constraints,
- increasing hierarchy order.

It directly addresses the question:

\[
\text{Which finite moment sequences are compatible with a stationary quantum state?}
\]

### What it does not provide

It does not:

- solve the nonlinear roots of a cumulant closure,
- continue branches between hierarchy orders,
- exploit the closure map generated by QuantumCumulants,
- manage multiple cumulant stationary branches,
- use cumulant roots as fast high-order predictors.

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

This work should provide the main physical validation layer.

A cumulant candidate \(x_n\) should be compared with the stationary feasible set

\[
K_n
=
\left\{
y:
A_ny=0,\;
y(1)=1,\;
M_q(y)\succeq0
\right\}.
\]

Useful diagnostics include:

\[
d_n(x_n)
=
\operatorname{dist}(x_n,K_n),
\]

and whether the candidate observables lie inside certified intervals

\[
\underline O_n
\leq
\langle O\rangle
\leq
\overline O_n.
\]

### Design implication

CumulantHomotopy should not treat a stationary polynomial root as automatically physical. It should attach moment-bootstrap evidence to every candidate.

## 2. Closest mathematical blueprint for hierarchy collapse

### Kuntz, Thomas, Stan, and Barahona: stationary moment bounds for the chemical master equation

**Reference**

J. Kuntz, P. Thomas, G.-B. Stan, and M. Barahona,  
“Rigorous bounds on the stationary distributions of the chemical master equation via mathematical programming” (2017).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1702.05468

### Main idea

The method constructs nested convex relaxations of the stationary chemical master equation using:

- exact stationary moment equations,
- moment positivity,
- support constraints,
- semidefinite or linear programming.

The output is not one arbitrary approximate stationary point. It is a sequence of shrinking bounds.

For an observable \(O\),

\[
\underline O_n
\leq
\mathbb E_{\mathrm{ss}}[O]
\leq
\overline O_n.
\]

In the unique stationary case,

\[
\overline O_n-\underline O_n
\longrightarrow 0.
\]

### Why it is close to CumulantHomotopy

This is the closest precedent to the statement that a finite hierarchy should collapse toward one stationary state.

The collapse is not expressed as

\[
\#\{\text{roots}\}\to 1,
\]

but as

\[
\operatorname{diam}(\pi_kK_n)\to0
\]

for fixed low-order observables.

### What it does not provide

It does not use:

- quantum operator algebras,
- cumulant closures,
- nonlinear branch continuation,
- metastable quantum branches,
- bosonic commutation relations.

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

This work provides the correct mathematical interpretation of physical collapse.

CumulantHomotopy should monitor:

\[
\operatorname{diam}
\left(
\pi_k\mathcal P_n
\right),
\]

where \(\mathcal P_n\) is the set of cumulant candidates that survive physical validation.

The goal is that all physically admissible branches agree in fixed low-order observables, even if formal polynomial roots remain.

### Design implication

The package should report observable envelopes and cluster diameters, not only one selected root.

## 3. Closest precedent for repairing a truncated hierarchy

### Krönke and Schmelcher: BBGKY truncation and purification

**Reference**

S. Krönke and P. Schmelcher,  
“The BBGKY hierarchy for ultracold bosonic systems” (2017).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1712.00819

### Main idea

High-order BBGKY truncations can violate:

- compatibility between reduced density matrices,
- positivity,
- conserved quantities,
- representability conditions.

The authors develop correction and purification procedures that minimally modify the truncated hierarchy while restoring important physical constraints.

They also show that increasing the truncation order alone does not guarantee stable or physical long-time behavior.

### Why it is close to CumulantHomotopy

A cumulant root may solve the algebraic stationary equations accurately while still being incompatible with any physical density operator.

The analogous correction problem is:

\[
\widetilde y
=
\arg\min_z
\|W(z-y)\|^2
\]

subject to

\[
M_q(z)\succeq0,
\]

\[
Cz=0,
\]

\[
A_{\mathrm{stat}}z=0.
\]

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

A BBGKY-style purification layer can:

- measure the distance from a candidate to a physical stationary moment sequence,
- repair small positivity violations,
- preserve symmetries and conserved quantities,
- reject candidates requiring large corrections.

Define the purification distance

\[
d_{\mathrm{pur}}(y)
=
\|\widetilde y-y\|.
\]

### Design implication

At first, purification should be applied only at selected continuation endpoints.

A later implementation may alternate:

\[
\text{Newton correction}
\rightarrow
\text{physical projection}
\rightarrow
\text{Newton correction}.
\]

## 4. Closest method that preserves one finite stationary solution

### Gupta, Mikelson, and Khammash: stationary finite-state projection

**Reference**

A. Gupta, J. Mikelson, and M. Khammash,  
“A finite state projection algorithm for the stationary solution of the chemical master equation” (2017).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1704.07259

### Main idea

Finite-state projection truncates the original Markov state space while retaining a finite linear generator.

The stationary problem remains

\[
L_Np_N=0,
\qquad
\sum_i p_{N,i}=1.
\]

Under appropriate stability assumptions, expanding the state-space truncation makes the finite stationary solution converge to the exact stationary distribution.

### Why it is important

This method avoids root proliferation entirely because the finite stationary equations remain linear.

If the finite generator is irreducible, the normalized stationary solution is unique.

### Quantum analogue

For bosonic quantum systems, the direct analogue is a finite Fock-space truncation:

\[
\mathcal L_N\rho_N=0,
\qquad
\operatorname{Tr}\rho_N=1.
\]

### Why it is not sufficient

The cost grows with Hilbert-space dimension, often much faster than a cumulant hierarchy.

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

Finite-state or Fock-space projection should be used as:

- a benchmark,
- a source of reference moments,
- an initializer,
- a tail and occupation estimator,
- a hybrid solver for a small strongly quantum subsystem.

### Design implication

CumulantHomotopy should compare its stationary output to a linear state-space truncation whenever such a calculation is feasible.

## 5. Closest method for constructing a better closure

### Rogers: maximum-entropy moment closure

**Reference**

T. Rogers,  
“Maximum-entropy moment-closure for stochastic systems on networks” (2011).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1103.4980

### Main idea

Given retained moments, the unresolved distribution is selected by maximum entropy.

The quantum analogue is

\[
\rho_{\mathrm{ME}}
=
\frac{
\exp
\left(
\log\rho_0+\sum_j\lambda_jO_j
\right)
}{
Z(\lambda)
}.
\]

Missing moments are reconstructed as

\[
\Phi_n^{\mathrm{ME}}(m)
=
\operatorname{Tr}
\left[
\rho_{\mathrm{ME}}(m)O_{>n}
\right].
\]

### Why it is close

It replaces an arbitrary high-order factorization with a unique variational reconstruction.

### What it guarantees

For admissible interior moments, it gives:

- a positive normalized reconstruction,
- a unique state within the chosen exponential family,
- a principled estimate of omitted moments.

### What it does not guarantee

It does not guarantee that the projected stationary equations have only one root.

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

Maximum entropy can be used as:

- an order-lift predictor,
- a positive reconstruction of a cumulant candidate,
- a unique selector in a convex feasible set,
- a closure regularizer,
- an alternative closure for comparison.

### Design implication

When lifting from order \(n\) to \(n+1\), compare:

\[
z_{\mathrm{cum}}
=
\Phi_n^{\mathrm{cum}}(x),
\]

with

\[
z_{\mathrm{ME}}
=
\Phi_n^{\mathrm{ME}}(x).
\]

The disagreement is a closure-uncertainty diagnostic.

## 6. Closest variational closure framework

### Bronstein and Koeppl: variational moment closure

**Reference**

L. Bronstein and H. Koeppl,  
“A variational approach to moment-closure approximations for the kinetics of biomolecular reaction networks” (2017).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1709.02963

### Main idea

Instead of imposing a heuristic relation between retained and unresolved moments, choose an ansatz family and project the exact dynamics onto that family through a variational principle.

Entropic matching appears as a special case.

### Why it is important

It reframes closure as:

\[
\text{choose a state manifold}
+
\text{choose a projection principle}.
\]

This is closer to a systematic reduced model than setting all cumulants above order \(n\) to zero.

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

It motivates future closure families such as:

- Gaussian manifolds,
- exponential families,
- mixtures of Gaussian or coherent components,
- positive phase-space ansätze,
- symmetry-adapted variational states.

### Design implication

CumulantHomotopy should allow multiple closure maps behind the same continuation interface.

## 7. Closest theory for correcting a closure manifold

### Gorban, Karlin, and Zinovyev: invariant-manifold methods

**References**

A. N. Gorban and I. V. Karlin,  
“Method of invariant manifold for chemical kinetics” (2002).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/cond-mat/0207231

A. N. Gorban, I. V. Karlin, and A. Yu. Zinovyev,  
“Constructive Methods of Invariant Manifolds for Kinetic Problems” (2003).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/cond-mat/0311017

### Main idea

A closure manifold should be approximately invariant under the exact dynamics.

For a manifold \(M_n\), define the invariance defect

\[
\Delta(\rho)
=
\left(
I-P_{T_\rho M_n}
\right)\mathcal L(\rho).
\]

A good closure minimizes this normal component.

### Why it is relevant

Zero high-order cumulants define a manifold, but not necessarily one that is close to invariant.

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

Invariant-manifold ideas can be used to:

- quantify closure quality,
- adaptively add moments,
- correct the closure map,
- choose between competing closure predictors,
- define held-out residuals.

### Design implication

The hierarchy should not grow only by total degree.

Operators should also be added according to their contribution to the invariance defect.

## 8. Closest uniqueness-preserving reduced stationary equation

### Mori-Zwanzig and stationary Schur-complement reduction

The full raw-moment hierarchy is linear.

Partition it into retained and unresolved moments:

\[
\begin{pmatrix}
\dot x\\
\dot y
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_{xx}&A_{xy}\\
A_{yx}&A_{yy}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
x\\
y
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

At stationarity,

\[
A_{yx}x+A_{yy}y=0.
\]

Formally,

\[
y=-A_{yy}^{-1}A_{yx}x.
\]

Substitution gives

\[
\left(
A_{xx}
-
A_{xy}A_{yy}^{-1}A_{yx}
\right)x=0.
\]

### Why this may be closer to the real objective than cumulant closure

The reduced stationary problem remains linear.

It therefore avoids the nonlinear polynomial root explosion.

### Main challenge

The unresolved inverse

\[
A_{yy}^{-1}
\]

is infinite-dimensional and expensive.

### Possible approximations

- Krylov subspaces,
- block Lanczos,
- continued fractions,
- rational approximations,
- auxiliary memory variables,
- finite unresolved sections,
- reset regularization.

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

This should be a parallel research backend:

```julia
ResolventClosure()
```

It can provide:

- a unique linear stationary predictor,
- a benchmark against nonlinear cumulant branches,
- a possible long-term replacement for hard zero-cumulant closure.

### Design implication

The project should compare:

\[
\text{nonlinear cumulant continuation}
\]

against

\[
\text{linear stationary resolvent reduction}.
\]

## 9. Closest theory for adaptive observable selection

### Brunton et al.: Koopman invariant subspaces

**Reference**

S. L. Brunton, B. W. Brunton, J. L. Proctor, and J. N. Kutz,  
“Koopman invariant subspaces and finite linear representations of nonlinear dynamical systems for control” (2015).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1510.03007

### Main idea

A finite observable basis closes exactly only when its span is invariant under the generator.

For the adjoint Liouvillian,

\[
\mathcal L^\dagger
\operatorname{span}(\mathcal B)
\subseteq
\operatorname{span}(\mathcal B).
\]

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

Define an approximate invariance defect

\[
\eta(\mathcal B)
=
\left\|
(I-P_{\mathcal B})
\mathcal L^\dagger
P_{\mathcal B}
\right\|.
\]

QuantumCumulants can identify which omitted moments are generated by \(\mathcal L^\dagger\).

The package can therefore add the operators that most reduce \(\eta\).

### Design implication

Adaptive basis construction may reduce:

- the number of variables,
- the number of polynomial roots,
- the cost of positivity tests,
- the required truncation order.

## 10. Closest theory for metastable branch mixtures

### Minganti et al.: Liouvillian spectral theory for dissipative phase transitions

**Reference**

F. Minganti, A. Biella, N. Bartolo, and C. Ciuti,  
“Spectral theory of Liouvillians for dissipative phase transitions” (2018).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.11293

### Main idea

Near a dissipative transition, one or more Liouvillian eigenvalues approach zero. Slow modes generate metastability and long switching times even when the exact stationary state remains unique.

### Xiang et al.: switching dynamics

**Reference**

Y.-X. Xiang, W. Li, Z. Bai, and Y.-Q. Ma,  
“Switching Dynamics of Metastable Open Quantum Systems” (2025).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.05202

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

Multiple stable cumulant roots may represent metastable components.

The correct stationary result may be

\[
\rho_{\mathrm{ss}}
\approx
\sum_jw_j\rho_j,
\]

where the weights satisfy

\[
Q^\mathsf Tw=0.
\]

The switching generator \(Q\) can be estimated from:

- phase-space simulations,
- quantum trajectories,
- instanton methods,
- Liouvillian slow modes,
- rare-event sampling.

### Design implication

The package should return either:

```julia
SingleStationaryBranch(...)
```

or

```julia
MetastableMixture(...)
```

It should not always force multiple roots into a single local branch.

## 11. Closest phase-space methods

### Wigner-Fokker-Planck stationary theory

**Reference**

A. Arnold, I. M. Gamba, M. P. Gualdani, S. Mischler, C. Mouhot, and C. Sparber,  
“The Wigner-Fokker-Planck equation: Stationary states and large-time behavior” (2010).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1010.2791

### Main idea

For a class of Wigner-Fokker-Planck equations, the authors establish:

- existence and uniqueness of a stationary state,
- positivity of the associated density operator,
- spectral gaps,
- exponential convergence.

### Why it is close

It is one of the strongest mathematical examples where a phase-space formulation retains a unique stationary quantum state.

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

It motivates:

- phase-space stationary predictors,
- quantum-to-classical continuation,
- spectral-gap diagnostics,
- direct comparison between cumulant and Wigner moments.

### Truncated Wigner

Truncated Wigner replaces higher Moyal derivatives by a second-order Fokker-Planck equation or stochastic differential equation.

It is useful for:

- semiclassical initial guesses,
- multimodal stationary distributions,
- metastable basin weights,
- natural fluctuation scales,
- quantum-to-classical benchmarks.

It is not a universal stationary certificate because long-time truncation errors can accumulate.

### Positive-P and gauge-P

These methods can represent many bosonic systems exactly in a doubled phase space, but can suffer from sampling noise and trajectory instabilities.

They are useful as independent reference methods where numerically stable.

## 12. Closest numerical root-control methods

### Sommese, Verschelde, and Wampler: equation-by-equation homotopy

**Reference**

A. J. Sommese, J. Verschelde, and C. W. Wampler,  
“Solving Polynomial Systems Equation by Equation” (2005).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/math/0503688

### Connection

Increasing hierarchy order changes both the variable set and the closure substitutions. It is not pure equation addition, but equation-by-equation homotopy gives a template for structured order continuation.

### Breiding, Rose, and Timme: Krawczyk certification

**Reference**

P. Breiding, K. Rose, and S. Timme,  
“Certifying zeros of polynomial systems using interval arithmetic” (2020).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.05000

### Connection

Local certification can show that one and only one nonsingular root lies near a numerical candidate.

This helps:

- merge duplicate numerical roots,
- certify branch endpoints,
- prevent artificial branch-count inflation.

### Duff and Lee: certified homotopy tracking

**Reference**

T. Duff and K. Lee,  
“Certified homotopy tracking using the Krawczyk method” (2024).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.07053

### Connection

Provides a route toward certified tracking of selected physical branches at low and moderate hierarchy size.

## 13. Closest methods for ruling out multiple physical roots

### Feliu: injectivity and multistationarity

**Reference**

E. Feliu,  
“Injectivity, multiple zeros, and multistationarity in reaction networks” (2014).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1407.2955

### Main idea

Injectivity conditions can rule out multiple positive steady states in broad polynomial systems.

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

For restricted physical regions, analogous Jacobian-sign or injectivity tests may prove that at most one physical root exists.

### Contraction theory

**Reference**

P. Cisneros-Velarde, S. Jafarpour, and F. Bullo,  
“Contraction Theory for Dynamical Systems on Hilbert Spaces” (2020).

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.01219

### Main idea

If the reduced dynamics is contractive in a suitable metric, it has a unique globally attractive equilibrium.

### Role in CumulantHomotopy

Search for a weighted metric satisfying

\[
J_n(x)^\dagger P+PJ_n(x)\preceq -2\alpha P
\]

over a physically relevant region.

If such a certificate exists, the truncated dynamics has one stationary point in that region.

### Design implication

Injectivity and contraction tests may provide stronger uniqueness evidence than root enumeration.

## 14. Synthesis

The closest complete architecture is:

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&\text{QuantumCumulants}
&&\text{for efficient high-order stationary candidates},\\
+{}&
\text{continuation}
&&\text{for bounded branch tracking},\\
+{}&
\text{Robichon--Tilloy}
&&\text{for quantum moment certification},\\
+{}&
\text{Kuntz et al.}
&&\text{for hierarchy collapse and uniqueness testing},\\
+{}&
\text{BBGKY purification}
&&\text{for minimal physical correction},\\
+{}&
\text{phase-space and metastability methods}
&&\text{for branch mixtures and the classical limit}.
\end{aligned}
}
\]

A parallel stationary-resolvent backend should investigate whether uniqueness can be preserved more directly by avoiding nonlinear closure.

## 15. Proposed method layers

### Layer 1: candidate generation

Use:

- physical parameter continuation,
- closure-consistent order continuation,
- maximum-entropy prediction,
- phase-space prediction,
- Fock-space reference states,
- stationary resolvent prediction.

### Layer 2: branch control

Use:

- a fixed branch budget,
- branch spawning only near singularity,
- clustering by low-order observables,
- local Krawczyk certification,
- injectivity and contraction tests.

### Layer 3: physical validation

Use:

- moment-matrix positivity,
- held-out stationary identities,
- positive-extension tests,
- BBGKY-style purification,
- stationary SDP bounds,
- energy and occupation constraints.

### Layer 4: stationary interpretation

Return one of:

1. a single converged branch,
2. a cluster of physically equivalent roots,
3. a metastable mixture,
4. an unresolved ambiguity.

## 16. Main research question

The central question should be formulated as:

> For a primitive open quantum system, does a bounded set of physically initialized cumulant branches, continued through hierarchy order and filtered by increasingly strong stationary moment constraints, converge in every fixed low-order observable to the unique stationary state?

A second, more fundamental question is:

> Can a stationary Schur-complement or resolvent closure retain uniqueness more reliably than zero-high-order-cumulant closure while remaining computationally competitive?

These two questions define the main nonlinear and linear research tracks of CumulantHomotopy.
