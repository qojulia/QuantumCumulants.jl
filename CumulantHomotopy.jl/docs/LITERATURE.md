# Literature map for CumulantHomotopy

_Last expanded: 12 July 2026_

This document collects literature relevant to the central CumulantHomotopy problem:

> Given an infinite linear hierarchy generated by a Liouvillian with a unique physical stationary state, construct finite approximations that keep the number of actively tracked stationary candidates small and converge, in fixed low-order observables, to the unique stationary state.

The finite cumulant equations may have many algebraic roots. The project should therefore distinguish:

\[
\text{all algebraic roots}
\neq
\text{physically admissible candidates}
\neq
\text{metastable components}
\neq
\text{the unique exact stationary state}.
\]

No single research area solves the whole problem. The most promising method combines hierarchy-aware continuation, physical realizability, adaptive observable selection, convex stationary constraints, phase-space information, metastable reduction, and optional linear resolvent closures.

## 1. Immediate quantum cumulant literature

### Plankensteiner, Hotter, and Ritsch, 2022

**D. Plankensteiner, C. Hotter, and H. Ritsch, “QuantumCumulants.jl: A Julia framework for generalized mean-field equations in open quantum systems,” Quantum 6, 617 (2022).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.01657
- https://doi.org/10.22331/q-2022-01-04-617

**Connection.** This is the symbolic and numerical foundation. CumulantHomotopy should use the actual closure map, canonical ordering, symmetry handling, adjoint relations, and moment graph produced by QuantumCumulants rather than treating the stationary equations as unstructured polynomials.

### Fowler-Wright et al., 2023

**P. Fowler-Wright, K. B. Arnardóttir, P. Kirton, B. W. Lovett, and J. Keeling, “Determining the validity of cumulant expansions for central spin models” (2023).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.04410

**Connection.** Demonstrates nonuniform convergence across odd and even orders, nonmonotonic error with system size, and incorrect large-system limits under some parameter scalings. The package must compare multiple orders and should analyze odd and even subsequences separately.

### Fowler-Wright, 2024

**P. Fowler-Wright, “Mean-field and cumulant approaches to modelling organic polariton physics” (2024).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.09812

**Connection.** Provides detailed high-order cumulant convergence studies in many-to-one open quantum systems and comparisons with matrix-product methods. Useful for benchmark design beyond single-mode Kerr systems.

## 2. Uniqueness of exact Lindblad stationary states

### Nigro, 2018

**D. Nigro, “On the uniqueness of the steady-state solution of the Lindblad-Gorini-Kossakowski-Sudarshan equation” (2018).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.06279

**Connection.** Reviews irreducibility, relaxingness, attractivity, and sufficient uniqueness criteria. CumulantHomotopy should evaluate these exact-system criteria where possible and distinguish failure of exact uniqueness from closure-induced multistationarity.

### Zhang and Barthel, 2023

**Y. Zhang and T. Barthel, “Criteria for Davies Irreducibility of Markovian Quantum Dynamics” (2023).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.17641

**Connection.** Gives algebraic irreducibility criteria in terms of the multiplicative algebra generated by jump operators and the Hamiltonian-dependent operator \(K\). This can provide a package-level statement that the original finite-dimensional Liouvillian is expected to have a unique faithful stationary state.

### Carlen and Maas, 2016

**E. A. Carlen and J. Maas, “Gradient flow and entropy inequalities for quantum Markov semigroups with detailed balance” (2016).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1609.01254

**Connection.** For detailed-balance semigroups, the exact dynamics is a gradient flow of relative entropy. This is the strongest setting in which a reduced closure may be designed to preserve a strict Lyapunov function and hence a unique stationary point.

## 3. General moment closure and known pathologies

### Kuehn, 2015

**C. Kuehn, “Moment Closure — A Brief Review” (2015).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1505.02190

**Connection.** Gives a cross-disciplinary overview and a geometric explanation for why rigorous closure convergence is difficult. It supports treating a closure as a chosen finite manifold rather than a neutral truncation.

### Schnoerr, Sanguinetti, and Grima, 2014

**D. Schnoerr, G. Sanguinetti, and R. Grima, “Validity conditions for moment closure approximations in stochastic chemical kinetics” (2014).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1407.8316

**Connection.** Shows negative variances, artificial oscillations, and multiple stationary moment solutions that depend on initial conditions. It explicitly recommends comparison with higher closure orders.

### Schnoerr, Sanguinetti, and Grima, 2015

**D. Schnoerr, G. Sanguinetti, and R. Grima, “Comparison of different moment-closure approximations for stochastic chemical kinetics” (2015).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1508.01737

**Connection.** Shows that the parameter region of physical validity depends strongly on the closure family. CumulantHomotopy should expose closure choice and compare more than one reconstruction when feasible.

### Naghnaeian and Del Vecchio, 2017

**M. Naghnaeian and D. Del Vecchio, “Robust Moment Closure Method for the Chemical Master Equation” (2017).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1707.00178

**Connection.** Constructs affine closure functions that are optimal in a worst-case sense and gives explicit approximation-error estimates. This motivates a linear closure or resolvent predictor that avoids nonlinear root proliferation.

### Bronstein and Koeppl, 2017

**L. Bronstein and H. Koeppl, “A variational approach to moment-closure approximations for the kinetics of biomolecular reaction networks” (2017).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1709.02963

**Connection.** Derives closures variationally and relates them to entropic matching. This is a direct precedent for defining a closure through projection onto a state family rather than imposing zero high-order cumulants.

### Andreychenko et al., 2015

**A. Andreychenko, L. Bortolussi, R. Grima, P. Thomas, and V. Wolf, “Distribution approximations for the chemical master equation: comparison of the method of moments and the system size expansion” (2015).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1509.09104

**Connection.** Combines moment integration with maximum-entropy distribution reconstruction and compares it to system-size expansions, including multimodal distributions. Relevant to phase-space and maximum-entropy predictors.

## 4. Entropy closures, realizability, and quadrature moment methods

### Rogers, 2011

**T. Rogers, “Maximum-entropy moment-closure for stochastic systems on networks” (2011).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1103.4980

**Connection.** Uses entropy maximization to remove closure nonuniqueness and inconsistency. Maximum entropy should be tested as a unique reconstruction of omitted moments and as an order-lift predictor.

### Alldredge and Schneider, 2015

**G. Alldredge and F. Schneider, “A realizability-preserving discontinuous Galerkin scheme for entropy-based moment closures for linear kinetic equations in one space dimension” (2015).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1501.03660

**Connection.** Treats realizability as a geometric domain that numerical updates must not leave. This suggests that continuation steps should monitor distance to the realizability boundary and optionally use realizability-preserving step control.

### Fan, Huang, and Wu, 2025

**C. Fan, Q. Huang, and K. Wu, “Provably realizability-preserving finite volume method for quadrature-based moment models of kinetic equations” (2025).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.18380

**Connection.** Shows how moment-matrix inequalities can be built directly into a numerical scheme. While the setting is kinetic transport, the philosophy is applicable to constrained continuation and branch pruning.

### Bandopadhyay, 2026

**S. Bandopadhyay, “A one-parameter family of realizability-interior closures for odd-order kinetic moment systems” (2026).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26032

**Connection.** Constructs closures that stay inside the realizability region instead of saturating its boundary. This suggests designing quantum closures with an explicit positive margin in moment matrices rather than accepting roots exactly on a fragile boundary.

### Buzek et al., 1998

**V. Bužek, G. Drobný, R. Derka, G. Adam, and H. Wiedemann, “Quantum State Reconstruction From Incomplete Data” (1998).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/quant-ph/9805020

**Connection.** A foundational use of quantum maximum entropy for incomplete observations. Relevant to reconstructing a positive density operator from retained moments.

### Swingle and Kim, 2014

**B. Swingle and I. H. Kim, “Reconstructing quantum states from local data” (2014).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1407.2658

**Connection.** Studies maximum-entropy reconstruction and certification from local data. Suggests comparing cumulant predictions against a maximum-entropy state compatible with low-order moments.

## 5. Truncated moment problems and semidefinite hierarchies

### Kuntz et al., 2017

**J. Kuntz, P. Thomas, G.-B. Stan, and M. Barahona, “Rigorous bounds on the stationary distributions of the chemical master equation via mathematical programming” (2017).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1702.05468

**Connection.** Gives convergent stationary moment and probability bounds and a computational test of uniqueness. This is one of the closest classical analogues to the desired hierarchy-collapse criterion.

### Robichon and Tilloy, 2024

**G. Robichon and A. Tilloy, “Bootstrapping the stationary state of bosonic open quantum systems” (2024).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.07384

**Connection.** Directly combines bosonic stationary identities with moment-matrix positivity to bound observables. This should be the principal external certification layer for bosonic CumulantHomotopy benchmarks.

### Frías Pérez and Acín, 2026

**M. Frías Pérez and A. Acín, “Local Certification of Many-Body Steady States” (2026).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.06487

**Connection.** Uses local reduced density matrices and semidefinite consistency constraints. It suggests validation that scales with observable locality rather than global Hilbert-space dimension.

### Navascués, Pironio, and Acín, 2008

**M. Navascués, S. Pironio, and A. Acín, “A convergent hierarchy of semidefinite programs characterizing the set of quantum correlations” (2008).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/0803.4290

**Connection.** Provides a canonical example of a complete noncommutative moment hierarchy and finite-level reconstruction criteria. The operator algebra differs, but the hierarchy architecture is directly relevant.

### Burgdorf and Klep, 2010

**S. Burgdorf and I. Klep, “The truncated tracial moment problem” (2010).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1001.3679

**Connection.** Establishes positivity and flat-extension ideas for noncommuting moments. Flatness or rank stabilization may provide finite-order certificates in special CumulantHomotopy cases.

### Blekherman and Fialkow, 2018

**G. Blekherman and L. Fialkow, “The core variety and representing measures in the truncated moment problem” (2018).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.04276

**Connection.** Gives representing-measure and positive-extension criteria for generalized truncated moment problems. Useful for formalizing what it means for a finite moment root to admit a physical extension.

### Magron, Forets, and Henrion, 2018

**V. Magron, M. Forets, and D. Henrion, “Semidefinite Approximations of Invariant Measures for Polynomial Systems” (2018).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1807.00754

**Connection.** Approximates invariant-measure moments and support through nested moment/SOS relaxations. This is the classical dynamical-systems counterpart to stationary Lindblad moment bootstrapping.

### Korda, Henrion, and Mezić, 2018

**M. Korda, D. Henrion, and I. Mezić, “Convex computation of extremal invariant measures of nonlinear dynamical systems and Markov processes” (2018).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1807.08956

**Connection.** Shows how a convex functional can select a specific invariant measure from a convex feasible set. This supports adding a strictly convex selector to a finite stationary moment set.

## 6. BBGKY truncation, N-representability, and purification

### Krönke and Schmelcher, 2017

**S. Krönke and P. Schmelcher, “The BBGKY hierarchy for ultracold bosonic systems” (2017).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1712.00819

**Connection.** Develops high-order cluster truncations and minimally invasive corrections that preserve energy, compatibility, symmetry, and necessary representability. It also demonstrates that increasing order alone does not prevent long-time instability.

### Pescoller, Eder, and Březinová, 2024

**E. Pescoller, M. Eder, and I. Březinová, “Projective purification of correlated reduced density matrices” (2024).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.13566

**Connection.** Provides a projective, least-invasive correction of defective reduced density matrices. The analogous CumulantHomotopy operation is projection of a root onto stationary, compatible, positive pseudomoments.

### Oña et al., 2026

**O. B. Oña et al., “Determining the ensemble N-representability of Reduced Density Matrices” (2026).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.06167

**Connection.** Uses purification and variational matching to test representability. It is not directly scalable for the target problem, but it reinforces representability distance as a meaningful diagnostic.

## 7. Invariant manifolds and thermodynamic projection

### Gorban and Karlin, 2002

**A. N. Gorban and I. V. Karlin, “Method of invariant manifold for chemical kinetics” (2002).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/cond-mat/0207231

**Connection.** Treats reduction as solving an invariance equation for a finite manifold and uses Newton corrections. This is a direct template for correcting a cumulant closure manifold.

### Gorban, Karlin, and Zinovyev, 2003

**A. N. Gorban, I. V. Karlin, and A. Yu. Zinovyev, “Constructive Methods of Invariant Manifolds for Kinetic Problems” (2003).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/cond-mat/0311017

**Connection.** Develops invariant grids, thermodynamic projectors, and dynamical correction. It supports adaptive manifolds and a closure-error measure based on the normal component of the exact vector field.

### Gorban and Karlin, 2005

**A. N. Gorban and I. V. Karlin, “Invariance correction to Grad’s equations: Where to go beyond approximations?” (2005).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/cond-mat/0504221

**Connection.** Shows how finite moment equations can be corrected by enforcing invariance instead of simply extending the list of moments. This suggests a correction step for cumulant closures that may be cheaper than raising order everywhere.

## 8. Mori-Zwanzig, memory, and stationary resolvent closures

### Chorin, Hald, and Kupferman, 2001

**A. J. Chorin, O. H. Hald, and R. Kupferman, “Non-Markovian Optimal Prediction” (2001).**

**Connection.** Establishes that exact elimination of unresolved variables generally introduces memory and noise. It explains why an autonomous cumulant closure can acquire spurious fixed points.

### Mori-Zwanzig projection literature

The projection-operator formalism gives an exact reduced equation with a memory kernel. At stationarity, splitting the exact linear moment hierarchy into retained and unresolved blocks gives

\[
\left(A_{xx}-A_{xy}A_{yy}^{-1}A_{yx}\right)x=0.
\]

**Connection.** This stationary Schur complement remains linear and therefore avoids a Bézout explosion. CumulantHomotopy should investigate Krylov, continued-fraction, rational, and finite-section approximations to the unresolved resolvent.

### Recent hierarchical memory work

Recent Mori-Zwanzig research develops hierarchical and short-memory approximations and analyzes the unresolved propagator.

- Example search anchor: https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.20457

**Connection.** Auxiliary memory variables may provide a finite extended phase space that captures the missing high-order feedback without forcing a nonlinear instantaneous closure.

## 9. Koopman, Carleman, and adaptive observable spaces

### Brunton et al., 2015

**S. L. Brunton, B. W. Brunton, J. L. Proctor, and J. N. Kutz, “Koopman invariant subspaces and finite linear representations of nonlinear dynamical systems for control” (2015).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1510.03007

**Connection.** Identifies finite invariant observable spaces as the ideal exact closure and emphasizes that useful finite invariant spaces are exceptional. CumulantHomotopy should measure approximate invariance rather than assume it.

### Cappelli and Succi, 2026

**L. Cappelli and S. Succi, “Lowest order Carleman linearization for steady state fluid flow simulations” (2026).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.23380

**Connection.** Investigates when low-order Carleman truncation recovers stationary states even if it is only approximate dynamically. This supports a separate stationary-kernel error analysis rather than relying on finite-time accuracy.

### Proposed observable-selection rule

For an operator basis \(\mathcal B\), define an invariance defect

\[
\eta(\mathcal B)
=
\left\|(I-P_{\mathcal B})\mathcal L^\dagger P_{\mathcal B}\right\|.
\]

**Connection.** QuantumCumulants can identify the omitted operators created by \(\mathcal L^\dagger\). Adaptive basis growth should add operators with the largest weighted contribution to this defect, not necessarily every operator of the next total degree.

## 10. Lumpability and Markov-chain reduction

### Geiger, 2022

**B. C. Geiger, “Information-Theoretic Reduction of Markov Chains” (2022).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.13896

**Connection.** Surveys coarse graining, model reduction, and lumpability. It provides language and metrics for turning several metastable cumulant branches into a reduced Markov process.

### KhudaBukhsh et al., 2018

**W. R. KhudaBukhsh, A. Auddy, Y. Disser, and H. Koeppl, “Approximate lumpability for Markovian agent-based models using local symmetries” (2018).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.00910

**Connection.** Quantifies approximate lumpability using information loss. Similar diagnostics could test whether a branch-level switching model is a valid coarse stationary description.

### Backenköhler et al., 2021

**M. Backenköhler, L. Bortolussi, G. Großmann, and V. Wolf, “Abstraction-Guided Truncations for Stationary Distributions of Markov Population Models” (2021).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.01536

**Connection.** Iteratively refines state-space regions relevant to the stationary distribution. It motivates adaptive branch budgets and adaptive Fock-space reference windows guided by stationary mass.

## 11. Finite state projection and stationary truncation theory

### Gupta, Mikelson, and Khammash, 2017

**A. Gupta, J. Mikelson, and M. Khammash, “A finite state projection algorithm for the stationary solution of the chemical master equation” (2017).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1704.07259

**Connection.** Provides stationary finite-state projection with convergence and error bounds under stability assumptions. The quantum analogue is a Fock-space or local Hilbert-space cutoff preserving a finite linear generator.

### Kuntz et al., 2019

**J. Kuntz, P. Thomas, G.-B. Stan, and M. Barahona, “Stationary distributions of continuous-time Markov chains: a review of theory and truncation-based approximations” (2019).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.05794

**Connection.** A broad reference on stationary truncation, convergence, and error control. Useful for defining tail and Lyapunov conditions for bosonic cutoffs.

### Infanger and Glynn, 2022

**A. Infanger and P. W. Glynn, “On Convergence of a Truncation Scheme for Approximating Stationary Distributions of Continuous State Space Markov Chains and Processes” (2022).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.11738

**Connection.** Uses regeneration to prove total-variation convergence of broad stationary truncations. It reinforces the importance of choosing truncations compatible with recurrence.

### Infanger, Glynn, and Liu, 2022

**A. Infanger, P. W. Glynn, and Y. Liu, “On Convergence of General Truncation-Augmentation Schemes for Approximating Stationary Distributions of Markov Chains” (2022).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.15167

**Connection.** Connects convergence to coercive Lyapunov functions and sublevel-set truncations. CumulantHomotopy should use occupation or energy bounds to define physically justified cutoffs and moment scales.

## 12. Phase-space methods and the quantum-to-classical limit

### Isar, Sandulescu, and Scheid, 1996

**A. Isar, A. Sandulescu, and W. Scheid, “Phase Space Representation for Open Quantum Systems within the Lindblad Theory” (1996).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/quant-ph/9605041

**Connection.** Separates the Wigner evolution into classical drift, quantum corrections, and open-system terms. This is a natural basis for a quantum-correction homotopy.

### Arnold, Fagnola, and Neumann, 2008

**A. Arnold, F. Fagnola, and L. Neumann, “Quantum Fokker-Planck models: the Lindblad and Wigner approaches” (2008).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/0806.2984

**Connection.** Bridges Lindblad and Wigner formulations with attention to stationary states and long-time asymptotics.

### Arnold et al., 2010

**A. Arnold, I. M. Gamba, M. P. Gualdani, S. Mischler, C. Mouhot, and C. Sparber, “The Wigner-Fokker-Planck equation: Stationary states and large-time behavior” (2010).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1010.2791

**Connection.** Proves uniqueness, positivity of the associated density operator, spectral gaps, and exponential convergence in a class of Wigner-Fokker-Planck models. This is a strong mathematical benchmark for phase-space stationary reductions.

### Ruostekoski and Martin, 2010

**J. Ruostekoski and A. D. Martin, “Truncated Wigner method for Bose gases” (2010).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1009.1073

**Connection.** A practical reference for stochastic Wigner methods and observable extraction. Truncated Wigner can provide semiclassical stationary predictors and metastable basin information.

### Drummond et al., 2003

**P. D. Drummond, P. Deuar, J. F. Corney, and K. V. Kheruntsyan, “Stochastic gauge: a new technique for quantum simulations” (2003).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/cond-mat/0309537

**Connection.** Reviews stochastic-gauge approaches that modify trajectory variance without changing physical observables. Gauge-P or positive-P can provide higher-fidelity reference moments than truncated Wigner where stable.

### Wüster et al., 2017

**S. Wüster, J. F. Corney, J. M. Rost, and P. Deuar, “Quantum dynamics of long-range interacting systems using the positive-P and gauge-P representations” (2017).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.06681

**Connection.** Describes exact stochastic phase-space simulation and its noise-time limitations. Useful for reference moments and scaling estimates, but not as a universal stationary solver.

### Plimak et al., 2001

**L. I. Plimak, M. K. Olsen, M. Fleischhauer, and M. J. Collett, “Beyond the Fokker-Planck equation: Stochastic Simulation of Complete Wigner representation for the Optical Parametric Oscillator” (2001).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/quant-ph/0108128

**Connection.** Demonstrates stochastic treatment of higher-than-second-order Wigner derivatives. Relevant to a quantum-correction homotopy beyond ordinary truncated Wigner.

### Braasch et al., 2017

**W. F. Braasch Jr., O. D. Friedman, A. J. Rimberg, and M. P. Blencowe, “The Wigner Current for Open Quantum Systems” (2017).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.04844

**Connection.** Gives a geometric phase-space picture of open Duffing/Kerr bistability. Wigner currents may identify inter-basin probability flow and inform metastable switching models.

### Oliva and Steuernagel, 2018

**M. Oliva and O. Steuernagel, “Quantum Kerr oscillators’ evolution in phase space” (2018).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1811.02952

**Connection.** Develops Wigner-current diagnostics for Kerr evolution and quantum corrections to classical shear. Useful for studying the quantum-to-classical crossover of the benchmark.

## 13. Metastability, switching, and dissipative phase transitions

### Minganti et al., 2018

**F. Minganti, A. Biella, N. Bartolo, and C. Ciuti, “Spectral theory of Liouvillians for dissipative phase transitions” (2018).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.11293

**Connection.** Relates slow Liouvillian modes, dissipative phase transitions, steady-state structure, and metastable eigenmatrices. Cumulant branches should be compared with the slow Liouvillian subspace where exact calculations are possible.

### Xiang et al., 2025

**Y.-X. Xiang, W. Li, Z. Bai, and Y.-Q. Ma, “Switching Dynamics of Metastable Open Quantum Systems” (2025).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.05202

**Connection.** Connects quantum-jump switching, small Liouvillian gaps, large deviations, instantons, and Arrhenius-like rates. This is directly relevant when multiple cumulant roots represent metastable basins rather than spurious states.

### Matsumoto, Baggioli, and Cai, 2025

**M. Matsumoto, M. Baggioli, and Z. Cai, “Dissipative quantum phase transitions monitored by current fluctuations” (2025).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.01136

**Connection.** Shows that output-current fluctuations detect dissipative criticality in Kerr and spin models. Trajectory observables could trigger branch spawning and identify metastable regions.

### Required interpretation

If branches \(x_j\) approximate metastable states, the stationary answer may be a mixture

\[
m_{\mathrm{ss}}=\sum_j w_j m_j,
\qquad
Q^\mathsf{T}w=0.
\]

The package should assemble mixtures in raw moments, estimate \(Q\) from phase-space or trajectory methods, and only then convert to cumulants.

## 15. Numerical algebraic geometry and root control

### Sommese, Verschelde, and Wampler, 2005

**A. J. Sommese, J. Verschelde, and C. W. Wampler, “Solving Polynomial Systems Equation by Equation” (2005).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/math/0503688

**Connection.** Introduces equations incrementally and exploits system structure. Increasing hierarchy order is not exactly equation addition because old closures change, but the strategy informs structured order homotopies.

### Breiding, Rose, and Timme, 2020

**P. Breiding, K. Rose, and S. Timme, “Certifying zeros of polynomial systems using interval arithmetic” (2020).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.05000

**Connection.** Krawczyk certification can prove local existence and uniqueness of nonsingular roots, preventing numerical duplicates from inflating the active branch count.

### Duff and Lee, 2024

**T. Duff and K. Lee, “Certified homotopy tracking using the Krawczyk method” (2024).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.07053

**Connection.** Extends interval certification to parameter homotopies. Relevant to certifying the physical branch path in low- and moderate-dimensional benchmarks.

### Root-control implication

The project should not enumerate all roots at high order. It should track a branch beam of bounded size, spawn branches only near singularity or path dependence, cluster roots by low-order observables, and prune using physical constraints.

## 16. Injectivity, contraction, and uniqueness of reduced fixed points

### Feliu, 2014

**E. Feliu, “Injectivity, multiple zeros, and multistationarity in reaction networks” (2014).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/1407.2955

**Connection.** Develops injectivity tests that preclude multiple positive steady states in generalized polynomial systems. Similar Jacobian-sign or injectivity criteria may identify parameter regions where a cumulant closure has at most one physically admissible root.

### Cisneros-Velarde, Jafarpour, and Bullo, 2020

**P. Cisneros-Velarde, S. Jafarpour, and F. Bullo, “Contraction Theory for Dynamical Systems on Hilbert Spaces” (2020).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.01219

**Connection.** Contractivity implies a unique globally exponentially stable equilibrium. CumulantHomotopy should investigate whether the reduced dynamics is contractive in a weighted norm near the physical region, and whether closure corrections can restore contraction.

### Curiel et al., 2024

**M. Curiel et al., “Positive Steady-State Varieties of Small Chemical Reaction Networks” (2024).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.09514

**Connection.** Studies positive parts of polynomial steady-state varieties. It provides algebraic-geometric methods and benchmark ideas for counting only physically relevant positive roots rather than all complex roots.

### Proposed use

For a candidate region \(\Omega_n\), search for:

\[
\frac{J_n(x)+J_n(x)^\dagger}{2}\preceq -\alpha I
\quad \text{for all }x\in\Omega_n,
\]

or more general weighted contraction inequalities.

If verified, this gives uniqueness and attractivity within the physical region even if the polynomial system has other roots elsewhere.

## 17. Quasi-free and exactly closed hierarchies

### Barthel and Zhang, 2021

**T. Barthel and Y. Zhang, “Solving quasi-free and quadratic Lindblad master equations for open fermionic and bosonic systems” (2021).**

- https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.08344

**Connection.** Gives exact closed covariance dynamics and steady states for quasi-free systems, plus block-triangular structures for broader quadratic models. These are ideal unit tests because the correct finite closure and uniqueness structure are known.

### Guo and Poletti, 2016

## 18. Design synthesis for CumulantHomotopy

The literature suggests four coupled layers.

### 18.1 Candidate generation

Use:

- physical parameter continuation,
- closure-consistent order continuation,
- maximum-entropy prediction,
- phase-space prediction,
- finite-section or Fock reference solutions,
- stationary resolvent or Schur-complement prediction.

Keep an explicit branch budget.

### 18.2 Candidate reduction

Use:

- symmetry and adjoint reduction,
- branch clustering in fixed low-order observables,
- injectivity or contraction tests in physical regions,
- local Krawczyk certification,
- aggressive rejection of clearly nonphysical candidates.

### 18.3 Physical validation

Use:

- moment-matrix positivity,
- positive-extension or flatness tests where available,
- held-out stationary identities,
- BBGKY-style purification distance,
- stationary SDP bounds,
- energy or occupation tightness,
- comparison with independent phase-space or finite-state calculations.

### 18.4 Stationary interpretation

Return either:

1. a single converged stationary branch,
2. a cluster of numerically distinct but physically equivalent branches,
3. a metastable mixture with switching weights,
4. an unresolved ambiguity with explicit diagnostics.

## 19. Most promising research hypotheses

### Hypothesis A: bounded branch complexity

A physical continuation with a fixed branch budget, combined with physical pruning, is sufficient over broad parameter regions even when the total algebraic root count grows rapidly.

### Hypothesis B: projected collapse

For a primitive Liouvillian, the diameter of physically admissible candidate clusters in any fixed low-order projection decreases with hierarchy order.

### Hypothesis C: realizability predicts convergence

Moment-matrix margins, purification distance, and positive-extension feasibility are stronger predictors of cross-order convergence than local stability of the truncated ODE.

### Hypothesis D: stationary resolvent closures reduce root proliferation

Approximating the unresolved stationary Schur complement produces a linear or rational reduced problem with fewer artificial branches than hard cumulant factorization.

### Hypothesis E: metastable roots should be mixed, not discarded

In bistable regimes, several stable cumulant roots correspond to metastable components. A phase-space or trajectory-derived reduced switching generator produces a unique stationary mixture that converges more reliably than selecting either branch.

### Hypothesis F: adaptive observables outperform uniform order

Adding operators according to weighted Liouvillian invariance defect reaches a target observable accuracy with fewer variables and fewer artificial roots than retaining every moment up to a fixed total order.

## 21. Ongoing scan protocol

The continuing literature scan should search for work in these categories:

- stationary quantum moment hierarchies,
- realizability-preserving quantum closures,
- high-order cumulant convergence and failure,
- positive-extension algorithms for bosonic moments,
- reduced Lindblad models preserving primitivity or detailed balance,
- stationary Mori-Zwanzig and resolvent methods,
- phase-space rare-event and switching-rate algorithms,
- contraction or injectivity certificates for polynomial closures,
- adaptive Carleman or Koopman bases,
- certified numerical continuation at singular roots.

New references should be added only when they change a design decision, supply a missing theorem, provide an implementable algorithm, or introduce a meaningful benchmark.
